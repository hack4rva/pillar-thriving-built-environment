# From Friction to Flow: Modernizing DPW Communications to Deflect Calls and Accelerate Storm Response

## Executive Summary

Public Works communications staff are currently operating as manual information routers, constrained by siloed systems, outdated knowledge bases, and a heavy reliance on phone-based resident inquiries. Analysis of 311 call center data reveals that solid waste and basic infrastructure issues dominate resident inquiries, yet poor digital self-service experiences force the majority of these interactions onto phone channels [1]. 

To alleviate this bottleneck, DPW communications require a bifurcated tool strategy: a resident-facing self-service portal optimized for high-volume transactional queries (like missed trash pickups), and an internal lookup console that aggregates real-time field data, GIS, and work order statuses. Furthermore, proactive storm communications can be drastically improved by leveraging text-to-subscribe alert systems, which have proven to capture 10,000 new subscriptions in just 48 hours during severe weather events [2].

## The Current State of DPW Communications

DPW staff are currently acting as manual routers for high-volume, low-complexity tasks that should be automated.

### Solid Waste as the 80/20 Volume Driver

A handful of tasks generate a majority of contacts; optimizing these yields outsized impact. In San Antonio, solid waste and basic infrastructure issues dominated the top citizen service requests in FY2016 [1]. "No Trash Pickup," "Dead Animal," and "Damaged Cart" requests far outnumber pothole repairs. Self-service tools must prioritize these specific solid waste workflows to achieve meaningful call deflection.

### The "Phone-First" Reality: Why 81% of Residents Still Call 311

Despite the availability of online portals and mobile apps, residents overwhelmingly prefer calling. In San Antonio, the 311 call center handled 285,050 calls in FY2016, representing 81% of the total service request volume [1]. This heavy reliance on phone channels consumes communications capacity and leads to poor customer service during peak times.

### Internal Friction: The Communication Gap

The success of DPW communications staff hinges on fast access to accurate, operationally-sourced data across solid waste, streets, and storm response. However, internal communication breakdowns between DPW management and frontline staff directly degrade the quality of information provided to the public. Staff often spend significant time chasing operations staff for project updates due to a lack of real-time information flow.

## Identifying and Closing the Digital UX Gap

Residents don't avoid digital tools because they are "non-digital"; they avoid them because the tools are missing essential features.

### The Search Bar Deficit: Why Web Portals Fail Navigation Tests

The lack of a search feature in web portals severely limits usability. In San Antonio, the 311 web portal contained approximately 133 service request options and an additional 172 information categories but did not include a search feature [1]. Users had to read multiple pages of categories to find the service request they were looking for, making the portal difficult to navigate and time-consuming [1].

### Mobile App Omissions: The Impact of Missing Top-10 Categories

Digital tools must have feature parity with the call center to be effective. In San Antonio, the mobile application lacked several top ten service request categories, including alley way maintenance, front/side yard parking, and bandit signs [1]. If a resident cannot report a common issue on the app, they will inevitably default to a high-cost phone call.

### Channel Shift Strategy: Moving from 2% to 20% Mobile Adoption

Digital adoption remains stagnant because the tools are not user-friendly. Improving the UX of digital channels is critical to reducing call center load.

Communications are only as good as the underlying data; inaccurate SLAs lead to resident frustration and repeat calls.

### Resolution Note Gaps: Why 87% of Complaints Remain "Open" Without Context

Stale Service Level Agreements (SLAs) and weak closure notes force manual chasing and callbacks. In San Antonio, of 1,501 complaints entered in FY2016, 87% remained open, and closed cases lacked resolution notes [1]. This lack of context prevents communications staff from providing accurate updates to residents.

### Bridging the Field-to-Office Divide with Real-Time GIS Access

To prevent communications staff from having to chase down operations colleagues, internal tools must provide authoritative, real-time answers. Implementing mobile work order updates for field crews and providing comms teams with view-only access to these systems will drastically reduce the time spent chasing project statuses.

## The Storm Playbook: SMS and Surge Communications

Proactive, automated alerts during weather events reduce the "surge" load on call centers and build long-term trust.

### Text-to-Subscribe: The St. Paul "STPAUL SNOW" Case Study

Because more than 90 percent of the public owns a mobile device, organizations should provide the ability to subscribe for information via text message [3]. The city of St. Paul uses text-to-subscribe to communicate with residents about snow emergencies. Subscribers can text "STPAUL SNOW" to 468311 to be added to the topic associated with St. Paul's snow emergencies [3].

### Capturing 10,000 Subscribers in 48 Hours: The Caerphilly Snow Event

Severe weather events are the most effective windows for audience growth. When severe weather hits, citizens come looking for information, presenting a huge opportunity to capture new subscribers [2]. For example, the last time it snowed in Caerphilly, the council gained 10,000 new subscriptions to winter weather alerts in just 48 hours [2].

### Multi-Lingual Outreach: Ensuring Equity in Emergency Alerts

Emergency communications must be accessible to all residents. St. Paul offers Snow Emergency text or email alerts in multiple languages, including Spanish, Hmong, Somali, Oromo, and English [4]. For instance, residents can text "STPAUL SNOW3" to 468311 to receive a text message in Spanish when a Snow Emergency is declared [4].

## Recommendations for Tool Design and Governance

Future DPW tools must prioritize address-based logic for residents and real-time work order feeds for staff.

### Resident Portal: Address-Based Logic and Top-Task Prominence

The UI must feature the highest-volume requests (missed pickup, cart replacement, dead animal) front and center. Residents need to easily look up their specific collection schedules and project impacts using address-based logic. Furthermore, the mobile app must contain the exact same request categories as the call center CRM to ensure parity across channels.

### Internal Console: Live Work Order Feeds and GIS Integration

Internal tools should feature live work order feeds integrated with mobile-first field apps so comms staff can see real-time status updates. A centralized dashboard to monitor the status of projects and contracts will prevent the need for ad-hoc status checks.

### Security Governance: Eliminating the "Ghost User" Risk in CRMs

Security and data hygiene are often overlooked in internal tools. San Antonio's CRM had 805 active users, but 659 had no activity, including 101 former employees [1]. Internal tools need strict role-based access control (RBAC) and periodic reviews to deactivate accounts upon HR offboarding, preventing unauthorized data access.
