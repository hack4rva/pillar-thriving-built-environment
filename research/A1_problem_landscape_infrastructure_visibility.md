# “Near Me” Isn’t Near Enough — Why Richmond’s Project Info Still Hides in Plain Sight

## Executive Summary

For a Richmond resident trying to understand why there are traffic cones on their street or when their water might be shut off, the City of Richmond offers a wealth of data but a poverty of answers. The core of Richmond’s infrastructure visibility problem is not a lack of information, but severe fragmentation, jargon-heavy communication, and mismatched update cadences. 

Currently, residents must navigate at least six separate portals—including the Capital Project Dashboard, the Department of Public Works (DPW) project pages, the Pavement Condition Index (PCI) map, the Street Closure Map, and the EnerGov permit portal—each with different scopes and update frequencies [1] [2] [3] [4]. While tools like the Capital Project Dashboard successfully offer address-based search and plain-language glossaries [3], other critical touchpoints rely on static PDFs, technical acronyms (UPC, NEPA, ROW), and instructions to "call ahead" to view plans in person [5] [6]. 

This fragmentation has real-world consequences. Recent local examples highlight the friction: residents on Cherokee Road waited years for safety improvements and only learned of impending fixes after media inquiries [7], while others experienced unannounced water shutoffs during Department of Public Utilities (DPU) meter replacements [8]. To solve this, Richmond must transition from publishing departmental data to answering resident questions, consolidating active works, closures, and utility projects into a single, address-searchable hub with strict data freshness standards.

## Problem Definition — Residents Can’t Easily Answer “What’s Happening on My Block?”

The fundamental difficulty for a Richmond resident seeking project information is that the city's digital infrastructure mirrors its organizational chart rather than the resident's physical geography. When a resident asks, "What is happening on my block?", they are forced to act as their own general contractor, piecing together clues across multiple departmental silos.

Multiple portals exist, but address search is inconsistently available where residents need it most. The Capital Project Dashboard explicitly supports address lookup and district filtering [3], and the PCI map is interactive [2]. However, DPW’s active construction pages are largely static, often directing residents to "call ahead to ensure the availability of appropriate personnel" or to review plans in person at City Hall [5]. Furthermore, update cadences mismatch daily-life questions. The city’s CIP Dashboard updates quarterly [3] and paving lists publish annually [2], which fails to help a resident navigating a day-of road closure or sudden utility work.

## Resident Journey — From “I Saw Cones” to “I Know What to Expect”

*Inference:* Based on the distribution of city resources, the typical user journey likely spans 4 to 7 steps across 3 to 4 different websites before a resident can piece together a clear picture of local impacts. 

1. **The Starting Point:** A resident typically begins at a search engine, the rva.gov homepage, or the RVA 311 portal. 
2. **The Routing Failure:** If they visit RVA 311's "Helpful Links," they are presented with a list of disparate tools (City Tree Map, Parcel Mapper, Permit Portal, Public Works Capital Improvement Projects) rather than a unified search bar [1]. *Inference:* 311 currently functions as a router to other departments rather than an answer engine.
3. **The Search for Address:** The resident must choose a portal. If they guess "Capital Projects," they may find the CIP Dashboard and successfully search their address [3]. If they guess "Paving," they are routed to an interactive PCI map or an annual PDF list [2].
4. **The Jargon Barrier:** If the project is in the design or public hearing phase, the resident often lands on a DPW page filled with technical codes (e.g., "UPC 115410", "STP-5B27(041)") and environmental review jargon [5].
5. **The Closure Check:** To find out if the road is actually closed today, they must locate the separate Street Closure Map, which is tucked into a "Your City. Your Services." FAQ page [4].
6. **The Utility Overlap:** If the cones belong to DPU or a private utility, the resident may hit a dead end, as these are not prominently featured on DPW's transportation-focused pages.

## Language & Comprehension — How Jargon Blocks Understanding

Technical language plays a massive role in the accessibility problem. When residents find a project page, they are immediately confronted with acronyms and federal/state codes that obscure the project's relevance to their daily lives. 

DPW project pages routinely feature headers like "U000-127-066, STP-5B27(041), (UPC 115410)" and describe processes using terms like "NEPA," "Draft Programmatic Categorical Exclusion," and "Section 106 and 36 CFR Part 800" [5]. Right-of-Way management pages require applicants to submit a "Maintenance of Traffic Plan (MOT)" and adhere to "VDOT Urban Manual guidelines" [6]. 

While the Capital Project Dashboard does offer a helpful "Glossary of Key Terms" (defining concepts like 30-60-90 Design Phases, ROW, and H&H Studies) [3], most residents encounter the acronyms on DPW pages long before they find the dashboard's glossary. This forces residents to interpret complex engineering and bureaucratic frameworks just to understand if their sidewalk will be closed.

## Update Cadence & Data Fragmentation

Timing breaks the resident experience. The questions residents ask are inherently immediate ("Why is my water off?" or "Can I park here tomorrow?"), but the city's primary data tools operate on macro timelines. 

The Capital Project Dashboard is updated on a quarterly basis [3], and the DPW paving program relies on annual fiscal year lists [2]. Meanwhile, DPW project pages frequently host static information, such as public hearing notices with comment deadlines that have long passed (e.g., "March 31, 2025" or "August 13, 2021") [5]. 

Furthermore, cross-jurisdictional projects add noise that residents must reconcile. Many city pages show VDOT funding tags and project numbers [5], while VDOT maintains a separate Richmond District projects site [11]. Residents cannot easily tell which VDOT-managed projects affect their specific block without cross-referencing multiple state and local databases.

## Richmond-Specific Case Studies

Recent local episodes demonstrate where public communication has broken down, eroding trust, alongside examples of where the city is attempting to improve.

### Communication Failures
* **DPU Water Meter Replacements (June 2025):** Residents reported being left "dry as a bone with no warning whatsoever" during DPU meter replacements. While some received door hangers, others reported the links on the hangers provided no specific timing answers. One resident reported a 7-hour delay in having their water turned back on due to a crew error [8].
* **East End Congestion (February 2026):** Neighbors in Richmond's East End reported that traffic and congestion from multiple overlapping construction projects have become the "new normal." Residents expressed frustration over projects lasting for years without clear communication on when they will be completed [12].
* **Cherokee Road Safety Fixes (October 2025):** Residents dealing with crumbling, hazardous road shoulders on Cherokee Road waited years for repairs. Despite DPW initially telling neighbors improvements would happen over the summer, a resident noted that the actual timeline for a November fix was "news to him" until a local news station inquired on their behalf [7].

### Success Seeds
* **RVA Builds Initiative:** In September 2025, DPW hosted "RVA Builds: The Infrastructure Information Initiative," a first-of-its-kind public open house at Main Street Station to give residents an insider's look at construction projects and improve transparency [13].
* **CIP Dashboard:** The interactive dashboard successfully centralizes multi-department projects and allows residents to search by address and filter by council district [3].

## Unknowns and Non-Verified Areas

Several critical aspects of Richmond's infrastructure visibility cannot be verified using publicly available information. These unknowns represent blind spots in the public's ability to hold the city accountable:

* **Utility Integration:** It is unknown if the city's Street Closure Map [4] comprehensively includes day-of closures caused by DPU (water/gas/wastewater) or private utilities like Dominion Energy.
* **Public ROW Map:** While the EnerGov portal allows users to look up specific permits [1] [10], it is unknown if there is a publicly browsable, map-first layer showing all active Right-of-Way closures and excavations.
* **Update SLAs:** There are no publicly verifiable Service-Level Agreements (SLAs) dictating how quickly DPW or DPU must update a project page once a construction milestone is reached or delayed.

## Assessment of Primary Friction Points

Based on the verifiable facts and labeled inferences, the primary friction points for Richmond residents are:

1. **Discoverability (No Single Front Door):** Residents must choose among multiple portals without guidance on which one answers their specific question. RVA 311 acts as a link directory rather than an integrated answer engine [1].
2. **Timeliness:** Quarterly and annual update cadences [2] [3] fail to meet day-of decision needs for closures, parking, and utility access.
3. **Comprehension:** Acronym- and code-heavy pages (UPC, NEPA, VDOT numbers) precede plain-language descriptions, forcing residents to decode engineering jargon [5].
4. **Fragmentation:** City (DPW/DPU), state (VDOT), and private utilities publish in silos. Applicant-centric permit tools (EnerGov) are not designed for public discovery [10] [6].

## Immediate Fixes (90 Days)

To rapidly cut resident effort and improve visibility, Richmond should implement the following low-lift, high-impact changes:

* **Elevate the Maps:** Promote the Street Closure Map [4] and the Capital Project Dashboard [3] to the top navigation of rva.gov and the RVA 311 homepage. Add a prominent "Search your address for active projects" search bar.
* **Plain-Language Headers:** Require a standardized, 100-word plain-language summary (What, Where, When, Impacts, Contact) at the very top of every DPW and DPU project page, pushing UPC codes and NEPA documentation to the bottom [5].
* **Timestamp Accountability:** Add a visible "Last Updated" and "Next Anticipated Milestone" date to every static project page to prevent residents from reading expired hearing notices.

## Structural Upgrades (6–12 Months)

To build a canonical source of truth for infrastructure visibility, the city must integrate its backend data:

* **Build a 311 Address Resolver:** Upgrade RVA 311 from a link directory [1] to an integrated search tool. When a resident types their address, it should return a single ranked list: ongoing/next-14-days closures, capital projects in construction, and planned utility work within a 0.5-mile radius.
* **Integrate Cross-Jurisdictional Data:** The CIP Dashboard [3] must ingest VDOT-in-city projects [11] and flag them as "VDOT-administered," applying the same plain-language impact fields.
* **Publish a Public ROW Layer:** Extract active Right-of-Way permits from EnerGov [6] and publish them as a live, public-facing map layer so residents can see exactly who is digging up their street and for how long.
* **Standardize Notifications:** Prevent DPU water shutoff surprises [8] by standardizing outbound notifications (door hangers with QR codes linking to live status pages, SMS alerts) that contain precise date windows and expected durations.

## Measurement & Governance

To prove these visibility upgrades are working, Richmond should track:
* **311 Deflection:** The percentage reduction in "construction near me" or "why is my water off" calls to the 311 call center.
* **Time-to-Answer:** The median number of clicks required to find a project near a specific address (Target: ≤ 2 clicks).
* **Data Freshness SLA Adherence:** The percentage of active construction projects updated weekly, and the percentage of road closures posted to the map on the same day they occur.
