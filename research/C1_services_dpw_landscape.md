# Richmond DPW, decoded: who does what and how to engage

## Executive Summary

The Richmond Department of Public Works (DPW) manages a vast and complex transportation network, but its public-facing structure often obscures how residents and partners should engage with it. DPW's transportation functions are highly centralized internally under the Engineering & Technical Services unit, which oversees everything from capital projects to traffic engineering. However, active transportation initiatives are spread across multiple divisions, creating a fragmented public narrative. 

While DPW has established strong foundational communication channels—anchored by the RVA311 system and the city's website—there is a distinct shift needed from episodic, project-by-project updates to continuous, centralized engagement. Furthermore, the jurisdictional boundaries between DPW and the Virginia Department of Transportation (VDOT) dictate maintenance responsibilities, with Richmond maintaining its own urban grid using state funds while VDOT handles the interstates. Unifying these communication streams and clarifying ownership will significantly reduce friction, speed up service delivery, and build public trust.

## DPW Structure and Ownership

Internally, DPW is organized into primary service units, with transportation assets managed primarily by **Engineering & Technical Services** (overseen by the City Engineer) and **Operations Management** [1]. The City of Richmond is responsible for a massive footprint: **832 center lane miles of street, 836 miles of sidewalk, and 83 bridges** [2]. 

Making this internal structure legible to the public is critical for reducing misrouted requests.

## Active Transportation Portfolio

Active transportation in Richmond is broader than just bike lanes, and the portfolio is split between the Office of Equitable Transit and Mobility (OETM) and the Transportation Engineering Division. The city has completed over **60 miles of separated bike infrastructure** [5].

### OETM Programs: Equity and Access
OETM manages programs focused on multimodal access and equity, including:
* Lighter Quicker Cheaper (LQC)
* Path to Equity
* Bicycle Program & E-Scooters
* Free Rides to Work
* Fall Line Trail & Rail (Main Street Station) [5] [6].

### Transportation Engineering: Street Safety Delivery
This division handles the physical implementation of safety measures:
* **Neighborhood Traffic Management Program (NTMP):** Receives over 1,000 requests annually to manage traffic speeds using calming techniques like curb extensions and speed tables [3].
* **Quick Builds Program (QBP):** Provides interim solutions using temporary signs, markings, and flexposts to address safety concerns faster than permanent infrastructure allows [3].
* **Vision Zero:** Aims to end traffic deaths by 2030 through safe systems design and roadway safety assessments [3].

**Takeaway:** Consolidating these diverse mobility programs into a single "Active Mobility" hub with a unified project pipeline will reduce fragmentation and increase the visibility of equity initiatives.

## DPW-City Communications Integration

DPW's public affairs function appears highly integrated with the central City of Richmond communications apparatus. DPW News posts utilize standard city press conventions and frequently highlight joint initiatives. For example, press invitations for the "Pothole Blitz" feature coordinated appearances by the Chief Administrative Officer (CAO), the Director of DPW, and the Director of the Department of Transportation [4]. 

## DPW-VDOT Relationship

The maintenance boundary between the City of Richmond and VDOT is dictated by Virginia state code. 
* **City Responsibilities:** As an independent city, Richmond qualifies for the Urban Maintenance Program (Code of Virginia § 33.2-319). The city maintains its own 11,900-mile urban street network with the help of state funds distributed by the Commonwealth Transportation Board [11] [12]. Richmond is also a member of the Urban Construction Initiative, allowing it to manage state and federally funded projects locally [11] [2].
* **VDOT Responsibilities:** VDOT maintains the interstate highways that run through the city limits [12]. 

**Takeaway:** Residents often do not know where city streets end and interstates begin. Embedding a "Road Responsibility" explainer using VDOT's Responsibility Master Route dataset will clarify jurisdiction.

## Data Quality and Transparency

A critical gap in DPW's current web presence is data consistency and lifecycle management. 
* **Inconsistent Asset Counts:** The Transportation Engineering page cites maintaining "over 490 intersections with traffic signals" in one paragraph, but later states "over 475 intersections" [3]. 
* **Stale Engagement Artifacts:** Project pages still host closed Survey123 links from 2022 and 2023 (e.g., SR 161 bike lanes) without providing outcome summaries or as-built updates [5]. 

**Takeaway:** Inconsistencies erode public trust. DPW must establish a single, timestamped asset count block syndicated across all pages and close the loop on public surveys by posting "What we heard / What we did" summaries.

## Research Outputs: Facts, Inferences, Unknowns, and Gaps

To directly address the specific parameters of the research request, the findings are categorized below:

### Facts (with source titles)
* **DPW Structure:** DPW is organized into Engineering & Technical Services, Operations Management, Administrative and Support Services, and General Services (Source: *About Us | Richmond*) [1].
* **Infrastructure Scope:** The city is responsible for 832 center lane miles of street, 836 miles of sidewalk, and 83 bridges (Source: *Construction Projects & Road Improvements | Richmond*) [2].
* **Active Transportation Programs:** Programs include Vision Zero, Neighborhood Traffic Management Program (NTMP), Quick Builds, Lighter Quicker Cheaper, Path to Equity, and the Bicycle Program (Sources: *Pedestrian, Bicycling and Trails | Richmond*; *NEW Engineering Transportation | Richmond*) [5] [3].
* **NTMP Volume:** DPW receives more than 1,000 Neighborhood Traffic Management Program requests every year (Source: *NEW Engineering Transportation | Richmond*) [3].
* **Emergency Routing:** Residents are instructed to call 9-1-1 for malfunctioning traffic signals or knocked-down stop signs, and 3-1-1 for all other maintenance (Source: *NEW Engineering Transportation | Richmond*) [3].
* **VDOT Relationship:** Richmond is an independent city that maintains its own urban streets using state funds via the Urban Maintenance Program, while VDOT maintains interstates (Sources: *Urban Program | Virginia Department of Transportation*; *Highways | Virginia Department of Transportation*) [11] [12].

### Inferences (clearly labeled)
* *Inference:* DPW's reliance on X (Twitter) as its primary social media call-to-action (frequently citing @DPW_RichmondVA in press releases) suggests a vulnerability to platform algorithm changes and potentially excludes demographics that prefer Facebook or direct email/SMS alerts.
* *Inference:* Because NTMP requests exceed 1,000 per year but emergency signal issues are responded to within 2 hours, DPW likely faces a severe backlog in processing neighborhood traffic calming requests, making public queue transparency highly necessary to manage resident expectations.
* *Inference:* The presence of joint press conferences featuring the CAO, DPW Director, and DOT Director indicates that while DPW executes the physical work, the City uses a centralized, top-down communications strategy for major infrastructure announcements.

### Unknowns
* The exact Service Level Agreements (SLAs) or turnaround times for non-emergency 311 requests (e.g., standard pothole repair outside of a "blitz" event).
* The specific internal workflow and approval process between DPW and the City's central Office of Communications before a press release goes live.
* The exact current status of legacy bike lane projects (like the SR 161 project surveyed in 2022/2023) as the website does not provide post-survey construction updates.

### Gaps between current communication channels and resident needs
* **Lack of a Unified Active Projects Map:** Residents need a live, geographic view of active work, detours, and milestones. Currently, project information is fragmented across disparate program pages and episodic news releases.
* **Closed-Loop Feedback:** Residents are asked to participate in Survey123 polls, but there is a gap in communicating the outcomes of those surveys. Stale surveys remain on the site without "What we heard" summaries.
* **Jurisdictional Ambiguity:** While VDOT clearly outlines maintenance responsibilities on its state site, Richmond's DPW pages lack a prominent, easy-to-use tool for residents to determine if their specific road issue should be routed to the City or to VDOT.
* **Over-reliance on Reactive Intake:** DPW relies heavily on residents reporting issues via 311, but lacks proactive, opt-in push notifications (like SMS work-zone alerts) for residents living in high-injury corridors.
