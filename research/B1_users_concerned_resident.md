# RVA’s Concerned Resident: Turning Project Chaos into Clear, Local Answers

## Executive Summary
Richmond residents face a fragmented information landscape when trying to understand local infrastructure projects. While the City manages a massive footprint of 832 center lane miles of streets and 83 bridges [1], residents often discover projects through a disjointed mix of physical signs, social media posts, and local news. The "Concerned Resident" persona is motivated primarily by immediate impacts: parking, access, noise, and utility disruptions. To serve this user, the City must transition from scattered updates to a centralized, hyper-local "Project Card" approach. Furthermore, equity considerations are paramount; with digital divides present, leveraging resources like the Richmond Public Library's laptop lending program [2] and ensuring Title VI/Limited English Proficiency (LEP) compliance [1] are critical steps to ensure all residents are informed.

## Why this Persona Matters — 832 street miles and 83 bridges make communication mission-critical
The sheer volume and cadence of Richmond’s infrastructure work means residents need clear, block-level answers fast—or trust and compliance drop. The city of Richmond is responsible for 832 center lane miles of street, 836 miles of sidewalk, as well as 83 bridges [1]. With Fiscal Year 2025 paving plans covering 170.5 lane-miles and costing over $15 million [3], the potential for daily disruption is high. 

### DPW’s current footprint and public touchpoints
Multiple official touchpoints exist, but residents see a patchwork, not a path. The Department of Public Works (DPW) utilizes project webpages, press releases, and social media accounts like X (formerly Twitter) to announce closures [4]. However, residents still turn to community forums like Reddit to ask, "Is there a place online that details what is being built where?" [5].

## Persona Sketch — “The Concerned Resident” in RVA
The Concerned Resident is a composite of Richmond locals who are suddenly faced with orange cones, heavy machinery, or "No Parking" signs on their block. They are not looking for a deep dive into urban planning; they want immediate, actionable answers to how the project affects their daily routine.

### Motivations and triggers: parking, access, noise, and safety
Alerts that resolve “Can I get in/out and when?” outperform generic project narratives. Residents are triggered to seek information when their immediate environment is altered. They want to know if they will be towed, if their water will be shut off, and how long the noise will last. 

### Barriers: fragmented info, jargon, hours, LEP/digital access
Gaps are structural and equity-related. Information is often siloed across different platforms or buried in technical jargon. Furthermore, residents without reliable internet access or those with Limited English Proficiency (LEP) face significant hurdles in accessing timely updates [1] [2].

## How Residents Learn About Projects — Which channels actually move information
Social posts and local news spark awareness; residents need a canonical link to act confidently. Currently, discovery is a mix of official channels, local news amplification, and community word-of-mouth.

### Discovery channels comparison
No single channel reaches all; cross-posting must anchor to a canonical Project Card.

| Channel | Evidence Source | Strength | Limitation | Best Use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DPW Construction Projects pages | rva.gov DPW Construction Projects [1] | Authoritative details, PM contacts | Hard to discover per-neighborhood | Canonical source of truth |
| DPW News page | rva.gov/public-works/news [6] | Official updates | Not hyper-local by block | Citywide status and milestones |
| DPW_X (Twitter) | @DPW_RichmondVA [4] | Fast, timely alerts | Posts expire in feeds | Day-of closures/detours with link |
| Local News FB (NBC12) | NBC12 FB [7] | Broad amplification | Partial details | Awareness bursts linking back |
| Reddit r/rva | "What's being built?" thread [5] | Community curiosity | Unvetted info | Redirect to canonical links |

*Takeaway: The City must establish a single source of truth (a Project Card) and use all other channels to drive traffic to that specific, hyper-local page.*

## What Information Residents Actually Need — The one-screen “Project Card”
Five fields answer 80% of questions: what/where, when/hours, impacts, who to contact, and the canonical link. 

### Required fields with city-aligned specifics
Include Project Manager (PM) contact information (as currently done on DPW pages [1]), daily hours with ordinance references, and a map with block limits. 

### Optional fields: benefits and funding IDs
PCI/benefit framing builds patience. Including the Pavement Condition Index (PCI) improvements or specific funding sources helps residents understand the long-term value of the short-term disruption.

## Timing and Noise — Set expectations with ordinance-aware messaging
Publishing daily hours and any extended-hours approvals reduces complaints and FOIA churn. 

### Richmond ordinance anchors
The City's Sound Control Ordinance defines "Daytime hours" as the period each day beginning at 7:00 a.m. and ending at 11:00 p.m. [8]. Construction activities are generally exempt from noise restrictions during these daytime hours [8]. 

### Failure case: off-season or late-night work without context
If work occurs outside these hours, or if "Daytime extended hours" are authorized [8], failing to communicate this proactively leads to immediate resident frustration and noise complaints.

## Seasonal Cadence and Exceptions — Paving April–November, but outliers happen
Residents tolerate variability if they see credible reasons and buffers. 

### Evidence: April–Nov program + Jan/March announcements
Each year DPW implements its paving program, which runs from April thru November [9]. However, exceptions occur, such as asphalt milling and overlay projects beginning in late January [7] or road closures in March [4]. 

### Action pattern: 2–7 day notice windows and parking specifics
Block-level “No Parking” timelines prevent towing angst and ticket disputes. Advance notice is critical for off-season or highly disruptive work.

## Equity Considerations — From Title VI/LEP to device access
Provide translations and non-digital access; don’t assume everyone scrolls X.

### LEP and Title VI on project pages
The city already signals support; operationalize it via translated Project Cards and hotlines. DPW project pages explicitly state compliance with Title VI and Title VII, offering special assistance for persons with limited English proficiency via the Capital Project manager [1].

### Digital access via libraries
Use libraries as distribution hubs for printed updates and QR help. The Richmond Public Library expanded its mobile device lending services to include Windows 10 laptops and WiFi hotspots to address the digital divide in the community [2].

## Channel Playbook — Canonical links plus cross-posting beats channel sprawl
Every alert starts and ends with the Project Card URL/QR.

### Templates by milestone
Reusable scripts speed publishing and reduce errors. Create standardized templates for announcements, pre-closures, day-of impacts, and project completions.

### Content calendar aligned to construction phases
Schedule posts around known milestones; sunset obsolete posts to prevent residents from finding outdated information.

## Feedback and Two-Way Communication — Make RVA311 part of every project
If projects start with 311, they should also listen via 311.

### Embed prefilled 311 forms and public Q&A snippets
Visible responsiveness reduces duplicate tickets. Numerous Capital Improvement Projects are based on information obtained from the RVA311 Service Request System [1]. Integrating 311 links directly into project updates closes the feedback loop.

## Benefits Storytelling — Use PCI and safety to justify disruption
Show the “after” to maintain goodwill. 

### Visuals: before/after and progress bars
Progress indicators reduce uncertainty. For example, communicating that DPW's hard work brought the percentage of streets with a “Paving Condition Index” (PCI) of Satisfactory or Good from 35% in 2018 up to 75% in 2024 [10] helps residents see the tangible benefits of construction delays.

## Measurement — Prove communications are working
Track reach, click-through to Project Cards, 311 sentiment, and complaint volume by block.

### Leading and lagging indicators
Aim to reduce “what’s happening?” calls and increase proactive compliance with parking restrictions.

## Risks and Failure Modes — What to avoid
Unanchored social posts, outdated PDFs, and missing LEP info create avoidable backlash.

### Mitigations checklist
Ensure every communication includes a canonical URL, date-stamps, translations, and sunset protocols for outdated social media posts.
