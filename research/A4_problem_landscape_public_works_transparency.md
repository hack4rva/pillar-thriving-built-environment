# Closing the Gap: Turning Fragmented Public Works Data into Resident-Centric Transparency

## Executive Summary

Public works information transparency fails in American cities not because of a lack of data, but because of structural mismatches between how cities produce infrastructure data and how residents consume it. Cities manage projects through fragmented, procurement-centric systems, while residents seek unified, address-based, plain-language updates. 

The research reveals several critical insights:
* **Utilities and Right-of-Way (ROW) drive hidden schedule risks:** 91% of agencies report utilities "Often," "Very Often," or "Always" impact urban projects, with 100% citing schedule impacts and 70% citing financial impacts [1]. Furthermore, when commercial ROW acquisition requires condemnation, 60% of cases take more than a year [1].
* **Data fragmentation erodes trust:** Major cities acknowledge that siloed data across ArcGIS, program pages, and grant documentation undermines public confidence. New York City's Comptroller explicitly noted that resulting data products are "not useful for project management, financial management, or transparency" [2].
* **Cities overinvest in low-yield communication channels:** Agencies still widely use newspaper notices and town halls, yet rank them among the least effective tools. Meanwhile, digital-first, measurable tactics are underutilized, and agencies lack robust measurement tools for communication effectiveness [1].
* **A proven bridge exists:** The Open Contracting for Infrastructure Data Standard (OC4IDS) connects contract-level disclosures with project-level data, addressing the gap that causes an estimated 10% to 30% of infrastructure investment to be lost through inefficiency, mismanagement, and corruption [3].

For cities like Richmond, the path forward requires shifting from compliance-driven notices to dynamic, resident-centric dashboards powered by unified data standards like OC4IDS and the U.S. Web Design System (USWDS).

## The National Transparency Problem in Public Works

Residents face joined-up problems—like neighborhood disruptions, detours, and utility shutoffs—but cities provide siloed answers. The dominant reasons residents cannot find infrastructure information are structural: fragmented systems, procurement-centric data, ineffective communication channels, and missing accountability metrics.

### Root Causes Blocking Resident Transparency

The failure of public works transparency stems from several documented operational gaps:
* **Fragmented Repositories:** Project data is scattered across ArcGIS maps, Capital Improvement Plan (CIP) PDFs, grant documentation, and procurement portals. 
* **Contract-First Data Models:** Cities track contracts and line items, whereas residents need project-level impacts and timelines.
* **Ineffective Communication Channels:** Agencies rely heavily on traditional methods. While public notices in newspapers are universally used due to legal requirements, agencies consider them highly ineffective [1]. 
* **Hidden Schedule Drivers:** Utility conflicts and ROW acquisitions are massive blind spots for the public. 91% of agencies report utility impacts are a significant problem, and 100% identify schedule delays as the primary consequence [1]. 

### Consequences Residents Feel Daily

When information lags behind reality, the civic costs are immediate. Residents experience unpredictability regarding detours and closures, leading to information asymmetry. This dynamic breeds frustration and erodes trust in local government. For example, public feedback in municipalities often highlights "frustration with poor communication about construction projects" [4]. Furthermore, poor communication and wrong utility information directly contribute to infrastructure bottlenecks [5].

## How Common Is Data Fragmentation?

Data fragmentation is a ubiquitous problem across American municipalities, evidenced by a wave of audits, comptroller reports, and active Requests for Proposals (RFPs) seeking to centralize disparate systems.

### Failure Cases and Their Signals

Without integration and governance, transparency dashboards underperform. In New York City, the Comptroller reported that weak capital data undermines infrastructure investments, stating that the data products are "not useful for project management, financial management, or transparency" [2]. Similarly, the NY State Comptroller highlighted that limitations in current capital project reporting constrain proper oversight and the review of committed funds [6].

### Operational Admissions in RFPs

Cities actively acknowledge these failures when procuring new systems. The City of Palo Alto's 2025 RFP for GIS services explicitly aims to centralize operations to "prevent duplicate and redundant GIS subsystems" and eliminate "disparate copies of data that inculcate a lack of confidence in data accuracy and completeness" [7]. This indicates that cities know the fix requires both technical architecture and strict data governance.

## Civic Costs of Poor Communication

While cities seldom quantify the exact financial cost of poor communication, federal guidance and mobility data show predictable, avoidable harms. 

### Treating Communication as a Mobility Intervention

The costs of implementation for meaningful public involvement vary, but the short- and long-range benefits must be considered when evaluating program budgets [8]. Communication is not just a public relations exercise; it is a critical safety and mobility intervention. Effective communication strategies, early planning, and close coordination are essential to ensuring project success and mitigating the massive costs of urban congestion [1].

### The Measurement Gap

Agencies currently lack robust measurement tools to assess the effectiveness of their communication strategies [1]. To close this gap, cities must start measuring outcomes before, during, and after construction. Metrics should include 311 call volumes, web engagement, detour compliance, and travel time reliability. 

## Production vs. Consumption: The Data Model Mismatch

There is a fundamental mismatch between how cities produce data and how residents consume it. Procurement data answers questions about suppliers, line-item costs, and award dates. Residents ask: "What is happening on my block, how long will it take, and who is responsible?"

### The Bridge: OC4IDS Connects Project and Contract Data

The Open Contracting for Infrastructure Data Standard (OC4IDS) bridges this gap. It combines contract-level disclosures using the Open Contracting Data Standard (OCDS) with project-level disclosure based on the CoST Infrastructure Data Standard (CoST IDS) [3]. 

Access to joined-up data is essential; without it, estimates suggest between 10% and 30% of infrastructure investment is lost through inefficiency, mismanagement, and corruption [3]. OC4IDS transforms paper-based processes into machine-readable open data, providing detailed guidance on publishing data at the project level, the contracting summary level, and the contract process detail level [9].

### Content Design Matters

Translating this data requires strict adherence to content design principles. The U.S. Web Design System (USWDS) mandates that agencies "keep communications simple," "write for the web" (expecting users to skim and scan), and "publish open code and data" [10]. Similarly, the Sunlight Foundation's Open Data Policy Guidelines require governments to proactively release information online, mandate data formats for maximal technical access (machine-readable), and create a central location devoted to data publication [11].

## National Standards & Playbooks Cities Can Lift-and-Shift

A practical stack of national standards exists. Cities do not need to invent new frameworks; they can adopt these selectively to achieve rapid improvements in transparency.

### Comparison of Infrastructure Transparency Standards

| Standard / Guidance | What it Covers | Where it Helps Cities | Notable Evidence / Claims |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **OC4IDS** | Project-level + contract linkage; lifecycle fields; persistent IDs. | Connects ArcGIS, CIP, grants, and procurement into a single registry. | 10-30% of infra investment lost without joined-up data; used on >25,000 projects [3]. |
| **OCDS** | Contracting processes (RFPs, awards, changes). | Publishing procurement data as open data. | Implemented by over 50 governments globally [12]. |
| **CoST IDS** | What to disclose at each project stage. | Serves as a project-level transparency checklist. | Measures transparency and quality of data publication [9]. |
| **USWDS** | Plain language, web UX, trust, and accessibility. | Designing resident-facing pages and alerts. | Mandates "write for the web" and publishing open data [10]. |
| **Sunlight Guidelines** | Policy scaffolding: inventories, formats, updates. | Drafting citywide open-by-default data policies. | Requires proactive release and machine-readable formats [11]. |
| **USDOT Promising Practices** | Meaningful public involvement methods. | Designing equitable outreach strategies. | Highlights short- and long-range benefits of meaningful involvement [8]. |

*Key Takeaway:* Cities should use OC4IDS as the backend data schema to unify their fragmented systems, while applying USWDS and Sunlight guidelines to the frontend presentation to ensure residents can actually understand the information.

## Applying the Evidence in Richmond

Richmond likely mirrors the national context: project data is fragmented across DPW ArcGIS assets, CIP PDFs, and RVA311 dashboards. Richmond can pilot a resident-first "Project Finder" by mapping existing data to OC4IDS fields and USWDS content standards.

### 30/60/90-Day Implementation Plan

* **30 Days (Inventory & Triage):** Conduct a comprehensive inventory of existing data holdings [11]. Select 10 high-impact priority projects. Assign persistent unique identifiers to these projects across all systems to establish a basic registry.
* **60 Days (Mapping & Design):** Map the priority projects to the OC4IDS minimum fields [3]. Create USWDS-styled project pages featuring plain-language status updates, milestone windows, and address-based search functionality [10]. 
* **90 Days (Integration & Loop Closure):** Integrate ArcGIS layers and CIP PDFs into the unified registry. Publish communication KPIs and integrate RVA311 categories to close the feedback loop, allowing residents to report issues directly linked to specific project IDs.

### Governance and Ownership

To prevent regression into silos, Richmond must name a specific project communications lead for each major project. About half of surveyed agencies report they "Always" or "Very Often" assign a project-specific individual to manage stakeholder issues [1]. Richmond should also establish a central standards owner responsible for data quality checks.

## Measurement & Accountability

Transparency itself must be transparent. Cities must publish how their communications perform and iterate based on resident usage and outcomes.

### KPIs and Public Scorecards

Agencies must move beyond tracking the number of flyers distributed. Effective KPIs include:
* 311 volumes categorized by specific project IDs.
* Web and app engagement metrics on project dashboards.
* Detour compliance and travel time reliability near work zones.
These metrics should be compiled into a quarterly public transparency report for all major capital projects.

## Risks, Costs, and Mitigations

Adopting a unified transparency model carries execution risks that must be managed proactively.

### Top Risks and Mitigations

* **Risk: Fragmented Ownership.** If DPW, Procurement, and IT do not collaborate, the data will remain siloed. 
 * *Mitigation:* Establish a central Project Management Office (PMO) responsible for the unified project data registry.
* **Risk: Duplicates Persist.** Leaving old ArcGIS maps and CIP PDFs active will confuse residents. 
 * *Mitigation:* Mark legacy pages as "superseded," implement auto-redirects, and actively identify and fix broken links (link rot) [10].
* **Risk: Privacy in ROW Acquisition.** Publishing exact parcel acquisition statuses can violate privacy or inflate real estate speculation.
 * *Mitigation:* Aggregate ROW statuses by project segment, redact Personally Identifiable Information (PII), and publish general process timelines rather than individual parcel details.

* *Inference:* Because agencies rank traditional public notices as highly ineffective yet continue to use them universally [1], cities are prioritizing legal compliance over actual resident comprehension.

* *Inference:* Richmond's public works transparency issues are likely not unique local failures, but symptoms of the national reliance on fragmented, legacy GIS and PDF-based reporting systems [2] [7].
