# Richmond DPW's Blind Spots: Closing Public Info Gaps Residents Feel First

## Executive Summary
Richmond's Department of Public Works (DPW) provides essential infrastructure services, but its public-facing digital presence suffers from fragmented visibility, stale content, and offline dependencies. While the City meets basic legal requirements for public notice, it falls short of providing the proactive, self-serve transparency that residents and journalists expect. Key project pages mix outdated notices with closed surveys, and high-demand programs like traffic calming lack public queues. Compared to peer cities like Roanoke, which offers centralized project dashboards with clear timelines, Richmond's approach creates friction, erodes trust, and likely increases the burden of Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) requests.

## Why These Gaps Matter — Reliability, Equity, and Trust
The biggest risks of Richmond's current communication strategy are missed participation windows, surprises during construction, and avoidable administrative burdens. When residents cannot find basic project timelines or statuses online, equity and trust erode. 

### Resident Journey Failure Points
A typical resident search path breaks down quickly due to dead ends, offline-only documents, and undated pages. For example, a resident navigating from the DPW homepage to "Construction Projects & Road Improvements" encounters mixed content, including a "Willingness to Hold a Public Hearing" notice with a response deadline of August 13, 2021, and a "Link to Survey – CLOSED" with no posted results [1] [2]. Furthermore, instructions often require residents to review documents in-person at City Hall or to "call ahead to ensure the availability of appropriate personnel" [1].

## Facts with Sources
The public record highlights several verifiable gaps in Richmond's DPW communications:
* **Construction Projects & Road Improvements | Richmond:** Contains a 2021 hearing deadline, closed survey links, and offline document review instructions [1] [2].
* **Neighborhood Traffic Management Program (NTMP) Final Report (2022):** DPW receives over 1,000 traffic calming requests per year and identified more than 125 neighborhood streets requiring further investigation in 2021, yet no public queue exists [3].
* **Vision Zero | Richmond:** Claims to provide a public dashboard tracking fatality and serious injury data prepared by the Richmond Police Department, but project pages do not consistently link to this data [4].
* **Vision Zero Action Plan (2021):** Specifies the development of an annual list of engineering projects that work toward the Vision Zero goal, which is not clearly centralized on the DPW site [5].
* **Virginia FOIA & VPPA:** State laws presume records are open upon request and require certain procurement disclosures, but they do not mandate proactive, real-time project dashboards [6] [7].
* **Roanoke CIP & Construction:** Roanoke provides a centralized hub with per-project estimated timelines (e.g., "November 2024 - July 2025"), maps, and portfolio scale data (215 capital improvement projects totaling more than $140 million) [8].

## Legal Must-Haves vs. Resident-Useful Transparency
Richmond appears to meet minimum legal compliance for notices, but this differs vastly from what is genuinely useful to residents.

| Legal Requirement | Resident-Useful Alternative |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Virginia FOIA:** Presumes open records upon request [6]. | **Proactive Publishing:** Auto-published project docs, decisions, and change orders in a searchable repository without requiring a FOIA request. |
| **Open Meetings:** Public notices and meetings are required. | **Meeting Summaries:** Post-meeting summaries, decisions taken, and "what changes" published within 7 days. |
| **VPPA:** Procurement records accessible upon request [7]. | **Financial Dashboards:** Per-project budget vs. spend to-date and contractor milestones visible on each project page. |

## Prioritized Gaps
To achieve the highest impact for residents, Richmond DPW should address the following gaps in order of priority:

1. **No unified, up-to-date project index:** Residents cannot easily discover if or when they are affected by construction. DPW needs a centralized "Projects Index" with filters and mandatory "last updated" stamps [1].
2. **Offline-only access to key project documents:** Requiring in-person visits to City Hall excludes many residents [1]. All materials should be digitized and posted online by default with ADA-compliant PDFs.
3. **No published NTMP backlog/queue:** Despite over 1,000 requests annually, there is no public visibility into traffic calming prioritization [3]. Publishing the backlog with expected windows is critical for expectation management.
4. **Stale pages and closed surveys:** Outdated notices (like the 2021 hearing deadline) fuel cynicism [2]. A quarterly content audit is needed to archive old pages.
5. **Lack of SLA/performance visibility:** While DPW encourages using RVA311, there are no visible Service Level Agreements (SLAs) or cycle times on the DPW site to manage expectations [3].
6. **Weak linkage to Vision Zero:** Project pages lack clear connections to Vision Zero priorities and crash data justifications [5] [4].
